# Grounding Assessment Categories

**When to read this:** Use this reference to understand the 9 dimensions of grounding and what to assess in each area.

---

Grounded Leader – Categories that manifest

1. Team Interaction & Collaboration

Focus: How present are you when engaging with colleagues, in meetings, or during any kind of group work?

Listening actively, noticing body language, understanding context & power dynamics.

Staying "calmly focused" rather than mentally drifting.

Speaking up clearly, asking clarifying questions, giving and receiving feedback.

2. Digital Communication & Notification Management

Focus: How well do you manage pings, emails, chat apps, and other digital feeds so they don't derail your attention or blur your priorities?

Turning off non-essential notifications and batching your email/Slack/Teams time.

Setting "no-notification" blocks for deep work.

Using "selective negligence" (i.e., intentionally delaying responses to nonurgent threads).

3. Task Focus & Execution

Focus: When you sit down to work on an individual deliverable or project, how often do you stay fully present (vs. rushing, multi-tasking, or operating on autopilot)?

Pausing before each task to connect it to your top priorities.

Single-tasking until a natural stopping point (vs. hopping between windows or tabs).

Noticing your mind wandering and gently bringing it back to the task at hand.

4. Prioritization & Strategic Alignment

Focus: How consciously do you link daily tasks to your longer-term goals and vision?

Starting your day (or week) by reviewing a written career roadmap or personal mission.

Ranking tasks by impact (instead of "just knocking out whatever's in front of you").

Saying "no" to low-value requests because they don't serve your strategic objectives.

5. Workload, Time Management & Boundaries

Focus: How effectively do you structure your workday (and your off-hours) so you neither burn out nor slip into constant urgency mode?

Having a clear "start-work ritual" (e.g., a brief check-in with priorities) and an "end-work ritual" (e.g., a quick review & plan for tomorrow).

Estimating realistic time blocks for tasks and protecting those blocks.

Setting (and enforcing) off-hours boundaries—knowing when to "turn off" notifications so you can recharge.

6. Emotional Resilience 

Focus: How grounded are you when stress or conflict arises—and how well do you pause to reflect on feedback (from others and from yourself)?

Emotional Resilience:

Staying calm and collected when a project goes sideways, instead of panicking or shutting down.

Framing challenges as opportunities for growth, using humor or perspective to diffuse tension.

Asking for help if you need it, and communicating clearly why a challenge matters (vs. reacting purely from emotion).

Feedback & Self-Reflection:

Truly listening when someone gives you feedback (without mentally rehearsing defenses).

Scheduling a 5–10-minute "reflection window" after a review or critique to process what you heard.

Writing down one concrete action you'll take from that feedback—rather than letting it sit in your head.

7. Self-Care, Recharge & Reflection

Focus: How often do you step away—intentionally—to recharge, reflect, and let creativity surface?

Blocking "white space" in your calendar (even 10- to 15-minute breathing breaks) instead of grinding for 8 hours straight.

Recognizing that your best ideas often emerge when you're away from your desk (walking, stretching, meditating).

Having a consistent self-care practice (e.g., a short mid-day walk, a mindfulness pause) so you avoid grinding into burnout.

8. Values, Purpose & Ethical Alignment

Focus: How frequently do you pause before decisions to check in with your deeper values and your team's mission—so you don't get pulled into reactive or misaligned work?

Before starting any project, asking yourself "Does this serve my (and my organization's) higher purpose?"

Speaking up if asked to prioritize speed over quality (or short-term gain over long-term reputation).

Noticing when an assignment conflicts with something you truly believe in (and having the courage to ask for clarification).

9. Adaptability & Change Management

Focus: When things shift unexpectedly—new leadership, sudden pivots, organizational reorg—how well do you stay grounded instead of slipping into panic or avoidance?

Pausing (even 15–30 seconds) to take a breath and orient yourself when change hits.

Asking clear, concise questions ("What does this mean for my existing priorities?") rather than reacting out of stress.

Breaking down the new situation into manageable next steps (vs. letting overwhelm take over).
